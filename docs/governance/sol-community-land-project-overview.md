Unified Sanctuaries / Sol is an evolving, land-based project rooted in:

* meeting basic human needs (food, water, shelter, belonging / community)  
* regenerative land stewardship  
* shared enterprise and livelihood  
* non-speculative, long-term community stability  
  * **Non-speculative:** The land/housing **isn‚Äôt treated like an investment vehicle** to flip for profit. Value increases aren‚Äôt meant to be captured privately (or at least they‚Äôre constrained), so the place doesn‚Äôt become a playground for real-estate speculation.  
  * **Long-term community stability:** The structure is designed so people can **count on staying** \- with predictable costs, clear rules for joining/leaving, and governance/ownership models that **protect affordability and continuity** across decades (not just whoever has money at the moment).

*We‚Äôre building a place to live and steward together \- not a property to cash out on \- and we want it to remain resilient, affordable, and coherent over time.*

This document exists to:

* share a living framework  
* make intentions clear  
* set shared expectations  
* establish pathways for belonging, contribution, and care

This is **not** a finished operating agreement.

## **1\. Residential & Living Realm (Private Dwellings \+ Shared Communal Amenities)**

This land is intended for **housing, daily life, and basic human needs**, while allowing for creative expression and livelihood pathways if residents choose.

**Includes:**

* Private dwellings (homes built or owned by individuals)  
* Space for trusted friends and collaborators to live on-site  
* A shared amenities zone that supports:  
  * People with minimal infrastructure  
  * Tiny homes / van travelers  
  * Visiting collaborators and guests

**Shared amenities may include:**

* Indoor / outdoor kitchen  
* Bathroom / showers  
* Laundry  
* Parking for vans, tiny homes, guests

**Residents *may* also choose to:**

* Host learning experiences  
* Share ways of living  
* Provide workshops, tours, or other offerings (participation in enterprise here is **optional)**

**Renting Homes on the Residential Side**

* Residents who build or steward homes **may rent them** out to those who are approved by current residents  
* Approval is guided by:  
  * the input and consent process of current residents  
  * alignment with our shared agreements, values, & policies  
  * the residential site‚Äôs current capacity (housing, resources, and stewardship needs)

This allows:

* flexibility for residents during travel or life changes   
* continuity of care for the land and infrastructure  
* Access and affordable housing for aligned people 

### **Residential Community Pool of Funds**

#### **Contributions**

* Working range (to be refined collectively): **5‚Äì15%** 

#### **Use of Funds**

* Maintenance and improvements of shared amenities  
* Infrastructure upgrades

**Shared amenities are:**

## **2\. Community Enterprise / Commons Land**

This land, adjacent to the residential land, is envisioned as being **owned and stewarded by the wider community through membership**, with clear structures for participation and decision-making.

This land will support **shared enterprises, food production / systems, cultural spaces, regenerative livelihoods, and service to the bioregion in which we reside.**

**Potential community land offerings include:**

* Farm & gardens  
* Farm stand  
* Caf√©  
* Retreat, healing arts center, & bathhouse  
* Campground  
* Event & gathering venue

**Key principle:**

* The land itself is not owned by individuals or businesses  
* Enterprises **rent or lease land/use rights** from the community (trust)  
* Each enterprise contributes 5-15% of earnings into a **shared community fund**

**This community pool of funds will support:**

* Land care & maintenance  
* Shared infrastructure  
* Community needs  
* Long-term contributor pay-outs (described below)

**Enterprises operating on the land:**

* Are responsible for their own taxes  
* Pay rent and revenue contributions as operating expenses  
* Maintains its own insurance (general liability, workers comp if applicable)  
* Operates as a legally distinct entity where required

**The land-holding entity / trust:**

* Maintains insurance covering land ownership and leasing

**Revenue contributions to the community pool:**

* May be treated as lease fees, program fees, or cooperative contributions depending on structure

## **Areas of Participation & Decision-Making**

The community is organized around **distinct pillars**, each with its own focus, rhythm, and governance.

**Example pillars include:**

* Farm including a farm stand, cafe, farm stand, nursery, and compost operation  
* Retreat & healing arts, including a bathhouse, a storefront, & a campground  
* Event & venue operations  
* Living space including private dwellings, communal space, &  amenities for visitors 

### **Decision-Making Is Role-Based**

* People **earn decision-making power over time** through involvement  
* Contributors participate in decisions **within the pillar they‚Äôre actively involved in and supporting**  
* No one votes on or influences areas they are not involved in, except for the anchor circle (more on that below)

This ensures decisions are:

* Informed  
* Grounded  
* Made by those closest to the work

## **Anchor Circle (Guiding Center)**

To ensure coherence across all pillars, there is a **guiding center called an anchor circle**:

* A **rotating, elected / nominated body**  
* Changes by:  
  * Season or other time period  
* Members rotate per their level of involvement in the community and per community input

**Role of the Anchor Circle:**

* Hold shared values and long-term vision  
* Support alignment across pillars  
* Address cross-pillar tensions or needs  
* Steward land-level and community-wide decisions

### **Double-Linking To Support Fluid Communication into and out of the Anchor Circle**

* Each pillar selects:  
  * One person to bring insights *from the pillar to the anchor*

* The anchor circle also designates:  
  * A liaison that represents each pillar to bring clarity *from the anchor back to that relevant pillar*

This creates **two-way flow**, transparency, and shared understanding \- rather than top-down control.

## **Trustees / Trust Managers (Distinct but Overlapping with Anchor Circle)**

* Trustees:

  * Safeguard the land and long-term intent  
  * Cannot personally profit from land appreciation

* Trust Managers:

  * Handle fiduciary and administrative duties  
  * May overlap with Anchor Circle members for continuity

### **Overlap Is Intentional**

* Some individuals may serve in **dual roles**, but:  
* No single group holds total control / checks and balances are embedded

## **Influence Is Tied to Active Participation**

Decision-making power is **earned and maintained through presence and contribution**.

### **Core Principle:** Influence follows involvement

* Members who are:  
  * Less present  
  * No longer actively contributing  
  * Stepping back from stewardship  
     gradually **lose decision-making influence**

* This includes:  
  * Voting rights  
  * Pillar-level influence  
  * Anchor-circle eligibility

Influence can be:

* Paused  
* Reduced  
* Re-earned through renewed engagement

This prevents:

* Dormant control  
* Legacy power without responsibility  
* Decisions being made by those no longer close to the work

## **Active vs. Dormant Membership States** 

Membership may exist in **states**, such as:

* **Active Member** ‚Äì full participation and influence  
* **Resting Member** ‚Äì retains belonging, reduced influence  
* **Former Member** ‚Äì no governance role, exit pathway applies

Transitions between states are:

* Transparent  
* Communicated  
* Guided by shared agreements, not punishment

This approach:

* Rewards care, not extraction  
* Keeps decision-making grounded in lived reality  
* Protects the land and community from drift  
* Allows people to step back *without destabilizing the whole*

It reflects a simple truth:

Communities stay healthy when responsibility and influence move together.

## **Option A: Community Land Trust (CLT)**

*(Best fit for the Community Enterprise / Commons Land)*

### **What It Is**

A nonprofit entity that:

* Owns land permanently  
* Separates land ownership from buildings/businesses  
* Exists explicitly to prevent speculation

### **Why It Fits**

* The CLT owns the **community land**  
* Enterprises lease land from the CLT  
* Members participate in governance  
* Land cannot be sold or flipped  
* Built-in affordability \+ permanence

### **Community Payouts**

* CLTs **can support payouts**, but:  
  * Not through land resale  
  * Through:  
    * Lease revenue  
    * Enterprise contributions  
    * Community funds

* Payouts are typically:  
  * Capped  
  * Time-based  
  * Book-value or contribution-based

### **Governance Fit**

* Strong alignment with:  
  * Anchor Circle  
  * Pillar-based decision-making  
  * Community fund governance

CLT \= *excellent* for land you want to protect for generations while supporting enterprises and community benefits

### **Governance Fit**

## **Option C: Cooperative-Owned Entity (Co-op Trust Hybrid)**

*(Best fit when you want member-ownership \+ payouts)*

### **What It Is**

A cooperative (or cooperative trust) where:

* Members hold ownership shares  
* Shares are tied to use and contribution  
* Profits are shared or reinvested

### **Why It Fits**

* Allows members to:  
  * Build equity over time  
  * Receive patronage or exit payouts

* Shares are:  
  * Redeemable at book value  
  * Not tradable on open markets

### **Community Payouts**

* Very strong support for:  
  * Ongoing payouts  
  * Exit payouts  
  * Monthly distributions  
  *   
* Members can often choose:  
  * Cash payout  
  * Reinvestment into community pool

### **Caveat**

* Co-ops alone don‚Äôt always protect land from speculation  
* Best when paired with:  
  * A trust or CLT that owns the land  
  * The co-op leases land long-term

**Bottom line:**  
Co-op \= *excellent* for equity, liquidity, and member benefit \- **not** ideal alone for land protection.

### **‚úîÔ∏è What a Co-op *Can* Do Well**

A cooperative **can**:

* Have **members that are different entity types**, including:  
  * Individuals  
  * For-profit businesses (LLCs, corporations)  
  * Nonprofits  
  * Other cooperatives  
  * Informal collectives (represented by individuals or entities)

* **Own or partially own other entities**, such as:  
  * LLCs (common)  
  * Subsidiary co-ops  
  * Operating companies

* Act as:

  * A **shared ownership layer**  
  * A **governance and coordination hub**  
  * A **profit-sharing / surplus-allocation mechanism**  
  * A **holder of shared assets** (non-land, usually)

This is often called a **multi-stakeholder cooperative** or a **federated cooperative model**.

### **What a Co-op Does *Not* Do**

A single co-op entity generally should **not**:

* Directly operate:  
  * A nonprofit program **and**  
  * A for-profit business **and**  
  * Hold land **and**  
  * Run all enterprises all inside one legal body

Why?

* Tax complications  
* Liability spillover  
* Conflicting fiduciary duties  
* Loss of clarity and protections

### **A multi-stakeholder cooperative sitting above diverse entities is:**

* Aligned with regenerative economics  
* Proven in Mondrag√≥n-style systems  
* Flexible enough to evolve  
* Legible to lawyers, lenders, and funders

### **Why A Co-op Fits Your Vision Perfectly**

Your system involves:

* Pillars with autonomy  
* Clear boundaries between land, living, and enterprise  
* Shared surplus without land speculation  
* Exit pathways and payouts

A cooperative can hold and coordinate many different kinds of entities as members \- including for-profits, nonprofits, and other co-ops. It works best as a shared ownership and governance layer, not as a single container for everything.

## **Membership Types (Working Language)**

These names are flexible, but intended to clarify pathways:

**Resident Members** ‚Äì live on the land; steward homes and shared amenities

**Pillar Stewards** ‚Äì actively contribute to a specific pillar (e.g. farm vs. retreat center, etc.)

**Enterprise Members** ‚Äì run or co-run businesses using community land

**Commons Contributors** ‚Äì support land care, infrastructure, or systems

### **1\. Resident Members**

* Live on the residential land  
* Steward their home and shared living infrastructure  
* Earn equity through contribution (see below)  
* Cannot sell land  
* Can sell their home to an approved incoming resident  
* Can rent their home to approved renters (approval by existing residents)  
* Acceptance of new members requires consent of existing residents

### **2\. Pillar Stewards**

* Actively contribute to a specific enterprise or land-based pillar (e.g. farm, retreat center, venue for events, living area, etc)  
* Participate in governance and decisions for that pillar  
* Build long-term stake through contribution and responsibility

### **3\. Enterprise Members**

* Run or co-run businesses on the community enterprise realm  
* Lease land/use rights from the community  
* Contribute a percentage of revenue to the shared community fund  
* Participate in pillar-level governance

### **4\. Commons Contributors**

* Support land care, infrastructure, maintenance, or shared systems  
* May not run businesses but contribute labor, skill, products / services, or coordination  
* Earn recognition and stake through contribution

### **Possible Decision-Making Methods (By Pillar Choice)**

Each pillar may choose one or blend several approaches, such as:

* **Consent-based decision-making \-** a decision moves forward unless there is a reasoned objection  
* **Consensus \-** used for values-level or long-term commitments)  
* **Voting \-** used for operational or time-sensitive matters)  
* **Delegated authority \-** trusted stewards empowered to decide within defined bounds)

The method chosen must be:

* Clear  
* Transparent  
* Communicated to all pillar members

## **How the Community ‚ÄúOwns‚Äù & Stewards**

The community‚Äôs relationship to the Community Enterprise & Commons Realm is based on **shared ownership, stewardship, and decision-making \- not individual land speculation**.

This means **the land is not an asset to flip, extract value from, or profit off through rising market prices.**

* No individual can buy the land cheap and sell it later at a higher price for personal gain  
* Land value increases (due to community effort, stewardship, or market shifts) **do not accrue to individuals**  
* Land is treated as:  
  * A shared, living commons  
  * A place to meet needs and generate livelihoods  
  * Not a speculative investment vehicle

### **What *Can* Be Exchanged**

* Homes, structures, or improvements people build  
* Businesses or enterprises (subject to community agreements)  
* Membership interests or equity **at book value or contribution value**, not market appreciation

### **What *Cannot* Be Exchanged**

* The underlying land itself  
* Land appreciation captured privately

### **Why This Matters**

* Prevents displacement  
* Protects affordability  
* Ensures the land remains accessible to future stewards  
* Keeps the focus on **use-value, not exchange-value**

*‚ÄúThe land is held for use and care, not for profit.‚Äù*

## **Exit Security / Liquidation Upon Exit & Pay-Outs**

This is a core value.

### **Who May Receive Pay-Outs:**

* Resident Members  
* Pillar Stewards  
* Enterprise Members  
* Commons Contributors (depending on contribution level)

### **Who Cannot Liquidate:**

* Short-term participants  
* Visitors  
* Provisional contributors who exit during blooming

### **How Pay-Outs Work:**

* Pay-outs come from the community fund  
* Distributed over time (e.g., monthly, quarterly, yearly, etc)  
* Designed to:  
  * Honor contribution  
  * Protect community continuity  
* Members exiting cannot sell the land they tended or lived on  
* Sometimes people *can* sell what they build, such as a home they built for themselves  
* Sometimes what is built is **integrated into the community** and cannot be sold independently  
  * In these cases:  
    * The community compensates contributors over time  
    * Pay-outs come from the **shared community fund**  
    * Payments may be monthly or staged  
    * No one can extract the full fund at once \- the land and community must remain healthy

The goal:

* No one leaves empty-handed  
* No sudden destabilization of the community  
* Fair payout over time rather than extraction in a moment

# **Legal, Trust, Governance, Grants, Banking, Accounting, and Liability Considerations**

### **Decision Framework \+ Recommended Options to Explore (VT project; phased growth; events \+ campground)**

This section is meant to help our team (and advisors) understand the major structural choices for Sol / Unified Sanctuaries, given our intention to create **one land project with two distinct realms**:

* **Residential / Living Realm:** primarily homes \+ friends visiting

* **Commons / Enterprise Realm:** public events, campground, retreats, farm stand/caf√©, and other enterprises

Because the Commons side will be higher activity and higher risk, we want structures that protect residential stability while keeping the Commons realm fundable, insurable, and operationally clear.

---

## **1\) The core design: one land, two realms, different governance**

Even though everything is part of one shared vision, these two realms have different needs:

**Residential Realm (lower activity / lower risk)**

* privacy, stability, home life

* shared infrastructure (roads, water/septic, microgrid)

* predictable finances (shared charges, reserves)

**Commons / Enterprise Realm (higher activity / higher risk)**

* public visitors, gatherings, campground operations

* more contracts, staff/contractors, permits, safety protocols

* higher insurance exposure

**Why this matters:** If we don‚Äôt design for different risk levels, the residential community can end up carrying the legal/financial burden of public-facing activity.

---

## **2\) The four layers we‚Äôre designing (simple ‚Äústack‚Äù)**

To keep this legible to community members, lenders, and funders, we can think in four layers:

### **A) Land-holding (mission protection)**

This is the ‚Äúland container‚Äù that **owns the land permanently** and prevents speculation (no flipping; land isn‚Äôt treated as a wealth-extraction asset). In practice, this is often a **Community Land Trust (CLT)**‚Äîa mission-driven landholding organization that leases long-term use rights through ground leases‚Äîor a purpose-built trust that holds land for a defined mission.

### **B) Operations (where risk lives)**

This is who actually runs public-facing functions (events, campground, caf√©, retreats). Most liability sits here, so we contain risk by using **separate operating entities** with strong insurance and clear contracts.

### **C) Governance (how decisions happen)**

This is how we make decisions in a values-aligned way while staying clear to outsiders. It includes realm-specific decision bodies and role-based influence (‚Äúthose doing the work steer the ship‚Äù), plus clear checks and balances.

### **D) Earned stake \+ exit (liquidity without securities)**

This is how people can contribute (cash and non-cash) and build an earned stake‚Äîwithout creating ‚Äúinvestment-like units‚Äù that look like securities‚Äîand still have clear exit pathways.

---

## **3\) Lending \+ grants: what funders and banks typically need**

### **Lenders generally want:**

* a clear borrower (who can sign, who manages money)

* predictable repayment sources (fees/leases, reserves)

* clear collateral (what secures the loan‚Äîland, buildings, lease rights)

* clear governance and budgets

### **Grantors generally want:**

* a clear public benefit (land stewardship, education, community resilience)

* a nonprofit recipient or fiscal sponsor (often required)

* confidence that grant funds won‚Äôt become private payouts

**Implication for Sol:** Grants tend to fit most naturally on the Commons/program side (education, stewardship, community benefit). Residential stability is often funded more cleanly through member contributions, housing financing, and shared charges.

---

## **4\) Liability \+ insurance: minimum containment (especially with events \+ campground)**

With public events and a campground, we need an explicit ‚Äúliability firewall.‚Äù The clean baseline is:

* the landholder maintains landowner/leasing insurance

* each operator (campground/events, caf√©, retreat programming, etc.) carries robust liability insurance and names the landholder as additional insured

* leases require insurance minimums and indemnity language

* we may use one dedicated ‚ÄúEvents/Campground Operator‚Äù entity so claims don‚Äôt spill into other operations

**Key idea:** Landholding can be mission-driven and stable; the higher-risk activity sits in the operating layer.

---

## **5\) Banking \+ accounting clarity (how we avoid confusion)**

Early clarity here prevents years of mess. At minimum, we separate books/accounts for:

1. **Residential shared pool** (common charges \+ reserves for roads/water/microgrid/shared assets)

2. **Commons community pool** (enterprise lease revenue, revenue share, program income, grant income if applicable)

3. **Each operating entity** (events/campground operator, caf√© operator, farm operator, etc.)

We also define revenue categories upfront so accountants and funders can understand them:

* ground lease / land stewardship fees (land use)

* common charges (shared infrastructure and reserves)

* program fees (retreats/workshops)

* lease payments (enterprises leasing space/use rights)

* revenue share to the commons pool

---

## **6\) The big structural choice: one landholder vs two landholders**

### **Option 1 ‚Äî One landholder \+ two realm leases \+ separate governance \+ separate operations**

**What this means:** One landholding entity owns the whole property, then creates two long-term lease frameworks:

* a Residential ground lease (living-realm rules and fees)

* a Commons master lease (enterprise realm rules and payments)

Each realm has its own governance body, and all high-risk activities are run by separate operating entities.

**Pros**

* simplest title structure (one owner)

* typically easier early for banking and baseline insurance

* lower formation complexity upfront

* still allows strong separation through leases \+ operating entities

**Cons**

* because one entity owns the whole parcel, we must be very strong on:

  * insurance requirements

  * lease boundaries and enforcement

  * strict bookkeeping separation

* some lenders may still perceive blended risk because events/campground exist on the same overall property

**Best when**

* we want to move quickly and keep early legal overhead manageable

* we plan to achieve most risk separation through operating entities and insurance

---

### **Option 2 ‚Äî Two landholders (Residential landholder \+ Commons landholder)**

**What this means:** Residential land is owned by one landholding entity, and Commons land is owned by a separate landholding entity.

**Pros**

* stronger asset firewall: commons risk is less likely to threaten residential land assets

* clearer underwriting story for lenders/insurers (stable residential vs high-activity commons)

* aligns with our intention that the Commons becomes high activity and higher risk over time

**Cons**

* requires legal separation of real estate interests (see Section 7\)

* more administrative overhead (two sets of governance, accounting, agreements)

* shared infrastructure still requires strong coordination agreements

**Best when**

* we are confident the Commons realm will be high-activity/high-risk

* we want maximum protection for residential stability and are willing to invest in cleaner legal separation early

---

## **7\) If we choose two landholders: three ways to separate the real estate in Vermont**

### **A) Subdivision (separate parcels)**

We create at least two legal parcels: Residential and Commons (and potentially more later).

**Pros:** clearest for lenders and insurers; strongest separation.  
 **Cons:** requires survey \+ municipal subdivision process; easements likely needed for shared access/utilities.

### **B) ‚ÄúSite condo‚Äù / Planned community (common-interest community structure)**

We define two ‚Äúunits/realms‚Äù within a recorded declaration and define shared vs limited-shared elements (e.g., shared road corridor; commons-only facilities).

**Pros:** can separate realms without a classic subdivision (depending on local practice); allows clear cost allocation by realm.  
 **Cons:** more documentation and association governance; in Vermont, true condos can have timing constraints around recording until certain construction completion‚Äîso a planned community approach is often more flexible for phased buildout.

### **C) Airspace lots \+ reciprocal easement agreement (REA)**

We create separate deeded 3D parcels/rights plus a robust agreement covering access, utilities, cost-sharing, and rules.

**Pros:** strong mixed-use separation without a typical condo/HOA feel; highly customizable.  
 **Cons:** most bespoke and legal-heavy; not all lenders are equally comfortable.

---

## **8\) Earned stake \+ exit: a clean approach that avoids securities risk**

We want people to contribute (including non-cash contributions) and build an earned stake over time‚Äîwithout creating investment-like ‚Äúunits.‚Äù

### **Recommended approach:**

**Residential:** people can own their home/structure, while land remains steward-owned. Exit happens through a **resale formula** (a clear pricing method that caps speculative appreciation while allowing the homeowner to recoup costs and approved improvements).  
 **Shared infrastructure and commons contributions:** track these as either:

* a **capital account** (a recorded balance of contributions that can be repaid under a written policy, often over time and subject to reserves), and/or

* a **member loan** (documented repayment terms)

**Why this helps:** it creates real liquidity pathways without ‚Äútradeable investment units,‚Äù and keeps the project aligned with non-speculative intent.

---

## **9\) Vermont planning note: phasing and permitting**

Because we intend 10+ homes and public-facing uses (events \+ campground), we should assume additional state-level review and permitting at some stage. We can still pilot at a smaller scale early, but we should design the structure so it can scale cleanly when we expand.

---

## **10\) Recommended options to explore first (best fit for our goals)**

Given our goals (values alignment, lender/grantor attractiveness, liability separation, clear banking/accounting, and earned stake without securities), we recommend exploring two pathways in parallel:

### **Path A (simpler start; strong separation through leases and operations)**

One landholder \+ two realm leases \+ separate governance bodies \+ separate operating entities.

### **Path B (maximum residential protection; best long-term fit if commons becomes high activity)**

Two landholders \+ a Vermont-appropriate real estate separation method (subdivision, planned community/site condo, or airspace \+ REA), plus separate operating entities.
